## A Note Before You Read

This document is not a feature list.  
It is not a requirements document.  
It is not a roadmap.

It is an attempt to articulate what C3 should *mean* — to the operations team, to the players, to Geekay as an organization — if we build it correctly over the next two to three years.

The best product decisions come from a clearly held idea of what you're actually building. Everything else — features, sprints, architectural choices — should flow from that idea. This document is that idea.

## Part One: The Story

### What you see today

Picture an Operations Manager — let's call her Rania — sitting down to start her week.

She has seventeen browser tabs open. One is a spreadsheet of contracts, colour-coded by hand, last updated by someone else two weeks ago. One is the HR system, which she cannot update directly. One is an email thread with an agent negotiating a player renewal. One is a PDF of a passport she uploaded to a shared drive somewhere. Two are FIFA/league registration portals. One is Teams, where a player is asking whether his visa has been renewed yet.

She knows the answer. She just cannot find it quickly enough.

This is not a people problem. Rania is excellent at her job. This is a tools problem. She is using infrastructure designed for a different era — file cabinets, email, spreadsheets — to manage a fast-moving, internationally-distributed, legally-complex, deadline-driven talent operation. Those tools are not designed for this. They were never designed for this.

C3, as it stands today, is a meaningful step forward. Contracts are visible. Renewal windows are tracked. The data exists somewhere that is not a spreadsheet.

But C3, as it stands today, is still a record-keeper. Rania still has to know what to look for. C3 still waits to be asked.

What follows is a picture of what C3 becomes when it stops waiting.

---

### What you see in two years

Rania opens C3. Not seventeen tabs. One.

The first thing she sees is not a dashboard full of numbers. It is a **Situation Room** — a view that has already done the morning scan, identified what matters today, and surfaced the three things that need her attention before anything else.

Not three hundred things. Three.

> **Ahmad Hassan — Contract Renewal**  
> Renewal window opens in 11 days. Last contact: 7 days ago. Agent is waiting on your revised offer. Contract expires: 2026-10-31.  
> *What do you want to do?*

> **Marcus Chen — Onboarding Incomplete**  
> 4 of 6 required documents verified. UAE residence visa not yet uploaded. Emirates ID not yet uploaded. Player is currently outside the UAE.  
> *Remind player · Request via agent · Mark as deferred*

> **Roster Eligibility — ESL Pro League**  
> Submission deadline: 5 days. 2 players have pending documentation. Provisional roster can be submitted now with a declaration of intent.  
> *Review players · Submit provisional roster*

These are not alerts generated by a cron job scanning a database. These are **operational moments** — situations that require a decision. C3 knows the difference between information and action. It surfaces the latter.

Rania clicks on Marcus Chen.

### The Onboarding Experience

Marcus was signed last Thursday. The moment his contract was countersigned in C3, the onboarding workflow began automatically.

Marcus received a secure, mobile-friendly link. His agent received a separate link. The club's PRO was notified that a new signing required visa initiation.

Through his link, Marcus uploaded his passport and filled in his personal details in a clean form — his name, date of birth, bank account, emergency contact, dietary requirements. The form was in English. In one section, he could switch to his native language for the form labels.

Through the agent's link, the agent uploaded the Player Information Form and a copy of Marcus's previous club's release letter.

C3 read every document.

From the passport:
- Full legal name: Chen Wei Jian, Marcus  
- Date of birth: 15 March 1999  
- Nationality: Singaporean  
- Passport number: E12345678  
- Expiry: 30 September 2027  

From the PIF:
- Name listed as: Marcus Chen Wei-Jian  
- Bank: DBS Singapore, SWIFT: DBSSSGSG  

C3 flagged two things before Rania ever opened the file.

First: the name. The passport uses Chinese name order ("Chen Wei Jian"). The PIF uses Western order with a hyphen ("Chen Wei-Jian"). These are the same person, but the discrepancy needs to be acknowledged because league registration systems are unforgiving about name mismatches. C3 presented both versions and asked: *Which form should be used for official registrations?*

Second: the bank account. It is a Singapore account. Most active UAE-based players receive salary in AED. C3 flagged this not as an error, but as an anomaly: *This player appears to be receiving salary in a foreign currency. Is this correct? If so, confirm the currency designation in the contract.*

Rania reviewed both flags in under two minutes. Confirmed the name format (PIF version, with a note explaining the passport ordering). Confirmed the Singapore account — Marcus is remote for the first three months while his visa processes.

She clicked **Approve Onboarding Record**.

Marcus Chen is now a verified person in C3. Every field is sourced and dated. Every document is linked to the field it populated. If someone asks in six months why the name is formatted a certain way, C3 will show them: *Name format confirmed by Rania Khalil on 2026-06-28 during onboarding review. Reason: passport uses Chinese ordering; PIF uses Western ordering; Western format selected for official registrations.*

The audit trail is not a box to tick. It is institutional memory.

### But here is where I want to challenge your onboarding idea.

The vision you described — upload a PIF, C3 reads it, extracts everything, flags uncertainty — is right. But I think it is still one step too conservative.

The PIF is an artifact of a world where C3 does not exist.

The PIF exists because organizations need to collect structured information from players, and they have no good mechanism for doing so. So they build a Word document or a PDF form, the player fills it in by hand or in a PDF editor, someone emails it somewhere, someone else types it into a system.

In the C3 future, **the PIF does not exist as a document**. C3 is the PIF. The player completes a C3-native intake — clean, mobile-friendly, available in multiple languages, with field-level hints explaining what each item is for and why it is needed. The data goes directly into C3 as structured, verified records.

The "PIF document" then becomes something C3 *generates*, on demand, from its own data — for league registrations, for visa applications, for insurance providers, for any external system that still requires a document. C3 stops consuming other systems' documents and starts being the system that produces them.

This is not a minor feature difference. It is a philosophical one. The PIF workflow you described is Document Intelligence: *C3 reads what other systems produce*. What I am proposing is System of Record: *C3 produces what other systems consume*. Both matter. Both should exist. But the long-term direction is the second one.

The transition looks like this:

**Year one:** C3 can import documents and extract data from them. You can still receive a PIF from an agent and have C3 read it.

**Year two:** C3 has its own player intake portal. New signings complete their information directly in C3. Documents are uploaded into C3, not as primary data sources but as supporting evidence. C3 reads them to verify what the player entered, not to populate fields the player hasn't touched.

**Year three:** C3 generates documents from its own records. The league wants a registration packet. C3 assembles it. The visa application needs a copy of the employment letter. C3 generates it, pre-filled, signed with the authorized signatory's digital signature, ready for submission.

### The Lifecycle View

The onboarding story is only the beginning. What C3 should ultimately track is the **entire professional life** of a person inside Geekay Esports.

That lifecycle has six chapters:

**Chapter 1: Consideration**  
A player is identified. Scouting notes are added. A trial is arranged. Initial contract terms are drafted. Nothing is official yet. But the record exists, and when the player is signed, nothing needs to be re-entered.

**Chapter 2: Onboarding**  
As described above. Documents, verification, identity establishment, system access, equipment provisioning, team integration.

**Chapter 3: Active Engagement**  
The player is on roster. Their contract is active. Their documents are current. C3 is watching in the background: visa expiry is tracked, contract milestones are surfaced, compliance with league regulations is monitored. C3 also knows the team's roster — which players are active, which are on loan, which are trialing. If the team submits a tournament roster that includes a player whose visa is expired, C3 flags it before submission.

**Chapter 4: Mid-Contract Events**  
Salary review. Role change. Team transfer. Injury leave. Performance clause triggered. Visa renewal. Passport renewal. Each of these is an operational moment that C3 should own — meaning: C3 knows it is coming before it happens, C3 orchestrates the workflow, C3 collects the required approvals, and C3 updates the record.

**Chapter 5: Contract Decision**  
The contract renewal window opens. C3 surfaces the player's performance data, their contract history, their market position. It does not make the decision — that is a human judgment. But it gives the decision-maker everything they need in one place, on one screen, without the need to pull up seven different systems.

**Chapter 6: Offboarding**  
The player leaves. Whether through non-renewal, mutual agreement, or release. C3 orchestrates the offboarding: visa cancellation request, equipment return checklist, exit documentation, final payment confirmation, competition ban period tracking if applicable. The player's record is not deleted — it is archived, and it contains the complete history of their relationship with Geekay. If they return in three years, that history is available.

### The Moment That Changes Everything

There is a specific kind of moment I want C3 to create for Rania — a moment that does not exist yet in any tool she uses today.

It is the moment when she realizes she is no longer tracking anything.

Not because the information disappeared, but because C3 is tracking it for her.

She does not have to check whether Ahmad's visa expires before the next tournament. C3 will tell her when the window to renew is approaching, before it becomes urgent.

She does not have to remember to send a renewal reminder. C3 will send it.

She does not have to reconstruct Marcus Chen's onboarding status from a dozen email threads and uploaded files. C3 knows his status, in real time, in one screen.

She does not have to build a spreadsheet of upcoming contract renewals every quarter. C3 already has it.

The feeling this creates is not productivity. Productivity is doing more work faster.  
This feeling is something different.  
It is **headspace**.

Rania has the mental space to think about strategy because the operational machinery is no longer consuming her attention. She can use C3's data to ask better questions: which players are undervalued on their current contracts? Which roles do we need to address in the next transfer window? What is the average time from signing to full visa clearance, and how can we compress it?

C3 makes her better at her job, not just faster at it.

That is the product I want to build.

## Part Two: Product Vision

**C3 is the operating system for talent at Geekay Esports.**

Not a contract management tool. Not an HR system. Not a document archive.

An operating system. The place where every professional relationship — from first contact to final exit — is managed, tracked, and understood.

C3 owns the operational lifecycle of every person connected to the organization. It surfaces what matters. It orchestrates what is routine. It remembers what humans would forget. And it preserves the judgment calls that only humans can make.

---

### The Three Pillars

**Continuity**  
Every piece of information about a person exists in one place, is always current, and carries its full history. No more reconstructing context from emails. No more "I think the contract is in the shared drive somewhere." The record is the truth.

**Intelligence**  
C3 knows the implications of what it stores. A passport with a 14-month expiry is not just a date — it is a visa renewal trigger, a tournament eligibility risk, an administrative task that needs to start in ten months. C3 surfaces these implications before they become problems.

**Accountability**  
Every decision has an owner. Every approval has a timestamp. Every document is linked to the field it supports. C3 does not just store decisions — it records who made them, why, and on what basis. When something goes wrong — and occasionally it will — you can trace exactly what happened.

## Part Three: Product Principles

These are the principles that should guide every product decision in C3's future. Not rules. Principles. When two options exist and neither is clearly better, these principles decide.

**1. Documents are inputs. Data is the product.**  
C3 reads documents so that humans do not have to transcribe them. But the goal is always structured, verified, queryable data. A document sitting in a folder is not useful. The data extracted from it, verified by a human, and linked to the records it supports — that is useful.

**2. The machine does the routine. The human makes the judgment.**  
C3 should never make a decision that has real consequences for a real person without a human in the loop. It can propose. It can extract. It can flag. It can draft. But approval — the moment of commitment — belongs to the human. This is not a limitation. It is the design.

**3. Surfaces action, not information.**  
There is no shortage of information in the world. The shortage is of clarity about what to do next. Every screen in C3 should answer the question: *what does this person need to do right now?* Not: *what data exists?*

**4. Consequences exist in time.**  
A contract end date is not just a field. It is the beginning of a renewal process that should have started 90 days before. A visa expiry is not just a date. It is a cascade of administrative tasks that have to happen in sequence to avoid a compliance failure. C3 thinks in terms of time and consequence, not just current state.

**5. The record is the institution's memory.**  
Geekay will change its staff. Operations managers will come and go. Players will return after years away. C3's records should be complete enough that someone who joins Geekay in five years can open any record and understand everything that happened, without needing to find the person who was there. If something is not in C3, it did not happen.

**6. Simplicity earns trust. Complexity loses it.**  
Every feature added to C3 has to earn its place by making the experience simpler, not more powerful. Power without simplicity creates tools that only experts can use. C3 is used by operations teams, not software engineers. Every screen should feel like it was made for one specific job, not for every possible configuration.

**7. Integration without dependency.**  
C3 should integrate with the world around it — league portals, visa systems, payroll platforms, communication tools — but should not depend on them to function. If the integration is down, C3 still works. Integrations are acceleration; C3's own data is the foundation.

## Part Four: User Experience

### The Three Roles

C3 serves three types of users, each with different needs and different relationships to the system.

**The Operations Manager**  
This is Rania. She needs situational awareness and process control. She wants to know what is happening and what she needs to do about it. She does not want to search for information — she wants C3 to bring it to her. Her primary screen is the Situation Room. She approves decisions; she does not create records from scratch.

**The Operations Coordinator**  
This person does the work that Rania approves. They initiate onboardings. They upload documents. They manage the day-to-day detail of contracts and amendments. They live in the person records, the contract records, the amendment panels. They need the system to be fast and comprehensive.

**The Player (or Agent)**  
This person is an occasional user. They access C3 through a secure, purpose-specific link. They see only what is relevant to them: their intake form, their documents, their contract status. They do not navigate the full application. C3 meets them where they are — on their phone, in their language, with clear instructions and no unnecessary complexity.

---

### The Design Language of Clarity

The current C3 design language is right. The tokens, the component hierarchy, the elevation system — these are correct.

What I want to add to the design philosophy is the concept of **progressive disclosure**.

Every screen should have one primary action and one primary piece of information. Additional context should be available but not immediately visible. The Situation Room shows three items — not thirty. The Contract Profile shows status first, then details on demand via tabs. The Onboarding record shows completion status first, then document details on demand.

This is not minimalism for aesthetic reasons. It is because the operations team is processing many things simultaneously. Cognitive load is a real operational cost. C3 should reduce it, not contribute to it.

## Part Five: Multi-Year Roadmap

### Year One — The Foundation is Complete (Current)

What exists now: the architecture, the design system, the screen layer, the service registry, the form framework. The application is fully functional in mock mode. The deployment infrastructure is ready.

What Year One adds:
- Sprint 6: Error boundaries, test coverage, accessibility, bundle optimization, real write operations (stage transitions, renewal decisions), SharePoint data layer
- Sprint 7: Person data from SharePoint, amendment data from SharePoint, full read/write cycle live
- Sprint 8: Power Automate write flows for all mutation operations, activity tracking live

**Year One ends when:** C3 is live in SharePoint with real data, real write operations, and a real user base. The application is stable and trusted.

---

### Year Two — The Intelligence Layer

Year Two is where C3 earns its name as a platform rather than an application. Three major capabilities are introduced.

**Document Intelligence**  
C3 gains the ability to read uploaded documents and extract structured data from them. This is not generic OCR. It is purpose-built for the documents that operations teams actually handle: passports, PIFs, contracts, visa stickers, bank letters, league registration forms. The system knows what fields to look for, knows the expected formats, and knows how to flag anomalies.

This requires: a document processing service (likely Azure AI Document Intelligence), a document type registry in C3, a field extraction configuration layer, and a human review and approval interface.

The review interface is critical. C3 should never silently populate a field from a document without the user seeing what it extracted and why. The review step is what converts document intelligence from a novelty into a trusted operational capability.

**Player Intake Portal**  
A C3-native intake experience for new signings and their agents. Mobile-optimized. Multilingual. Purpose-built for each document type — personal information, bank details, emergency contacts, consent declarations. When a player completes their intake, C3 verifies what they entered against any documents they upload.

The PIF, in its traditional form, is deprecated.

**Operational Continuity**  
C3 gains awareness of time and consequence. It knows that a visa expiry requires action 10 weeks before the date. It knows that a contract renewal window opens 90 days before expiry. It knows that a tournament eligibility submission requires verified documentation for every listed player.

These are not alerts. They are **operational triggers** — the beginning of a workflow, not just a notification. When a visa renewal trigger fires, C3 does not just tell Rania. It opens a visa renewal workflow, assigns tasks to the PRO, creates a checklist, and tracks completion.

---

### Year Three — The Predictive Layer

Year Three is where C3 becomes genuinely intelligent about the organization, not just about individual records.

**Roster Intelligence**  
C3 understands rosters, not just players. It knows which players are on which teams, which are on loan, which are trialing. It understands tournament rosters and eligibility windows. When a manager is building a tournament submission, C3 can validate it instantly: all players verified, all documents current, all league registration requirements met.

**Renewal Intelligence**  
C3 can analyze the portfolio of contracts and surface renewal risk at the organizational level. Which players are coming off contract in the next two quarters? Which have expressed interest in staying? Which are likely to attract external offers based on their performance trajectory? C3 does not make these decisions, but it makes them legible.

**Predictive Compliance**  
C3 tracks regulatory requirements across leagues and jurisdictions and maps them to individual records. If a league changes its eligibility rules, C3 can immediately identify which players are affected and what action is required.

**The C3 Intelligence Hub — Mature**  
The Intelligence Hub, currently a placeholder, becomes the analytical surface of the organization. Contract spend by team, by game title, by nationality. Renewal conversion rates. Average onboarding time. Visa processing duration by nationality. These are not vanity metrics. They are operational KPIs that help Geekay run a more efficient, more informed talent operation.

---

### The Document Generation Layer (Year Three+)

The final transformation. C3 stops consuming documents from external systems and starts generating them.

A league registration portal needs a player's contract details? C3 generates the registration form from its own records, pre-filled, ready to submit.

A visa application needs an employment letter? C3 generates it, formatted correctly for the UAE immigration authority, signed with the authorized signatory's credentials.

An agent wants a contract summary? C3 generates a clean summary document with only the fields the agent is authorized to see.

This is not a document management feature. It is the completion of a loop that starts with data capture and ends with data output. When this layer is complete, the document is no longer the unit of truth. The record is.

---

## Closing Thought

There is a version of C3 that is a very good contract management tool. It stores contracts, tracks renewals, logs amendments. It is useful. It saves time. It is better than a spreadsheet.

I do not think that version is the right ambition.

The right ambition is a version of C3 that changes what it means to work in operations at an esports organization. Where the cognitive load of managing fifteen international contracts is not heavier than managing two. Where a new signing's entire professional status — visa, contract, roster, documents, history — is visible in one screen, accurate to today, and intelligible to anyone who opens it.

Where Rania can walk into a board meeting and say: "Here is exactly where we stand on every player. Here are the decisions we need to make in the next ninety days. Here is what happens if we do not make them."

And she gets that from one screen.

That is the product.

The code we write is in service of that idea.  
Everything else is implementation detail.
